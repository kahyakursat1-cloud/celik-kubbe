%% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\abstract{%
Counter-unmanned aerial vehicle (C-UAV) systems require robust multi-sensor
fusion and operator-facing explainability for contested environments.
This paper presents ContextFusion, a context-sensitive explainable AI (XAI)
threat scoring framework with confidence-weighted cross-modal fusion for
multi-sensor C-UAV systems.
ContextFusion fuses YOLOv11m camera detections with X-band FMCW radar
measurements via a log-linear MAP fusion model; context-sensitive threat
weights ($r$-, $v$-, $c$-factors) adapt to scene complexity and sensor
conditions, and SHAP attribution explains per-track decision drivers to
operators in real time.
A physics-based radar simulator (Swerling Case~1 RCS fluctuations,
flat-earth multipath, CA-CFAR) enables reproducible evaluation without
physical hardware.
Monte Carlo experiments ($N=50$ runs per scenario, $N_\text{total}=200$)
demonstrate statistically significant performance
advantages over three camera-only baseline trackers (DeepSORT, ByteTrack,
IMM-Kalman): Bonferroni-corrected Wilcoxon signed-rank tests yield
$p_\text{corr} < 0.00125$ in 12 of 16 pairwise comparisons with large
effect sizes (Cliff's $\delta > 0.70$).
Mean MOTA of $0.645$ [95\% CI: 0.618--0.672] is achieved across all
scenarios, versus $-0.038$ for DeepSORT and $-1.956$ for ByteTrack.
ContextFusion is presented as a \emph{reproducible research framework}
for explainable C-UAV evaluation, not a deployment-ready platform;
hardware validation and real-data calibration remain future work, and
the codebase will be released upon acceptance.
}

\keyword{counter-UAV; sensor fusion; explainable AI; threat scoring;
         multi-object tracking; radar simulation;
         confidence-weighted fusion; context-sensitive XAI}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The proliferation of commercially available unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) has
created significant security challenges for critical infrastructure protection,
airspace management, and military operations
\cite{ref_uav_threat_survey,ref_cuav_review}.
Counter-UAV (C-UAV) systems must detect, classify, track, and neutralize
small aerial threats in real time, often under adverse conditions including
sensor noise, cluttered environments, and electronic interference
\cite{ref_drone_detection_dl,ref_small_uav_detection}.

Multi-sensor fusion approaches combining radar and electro-optical (EO)
sensors offer complementary advantages: radar provides all-weather range and
velocity measurement, while camera-based deep learning excels at fine-grained
classification \cite{ref_radar_camera_fusion,ref_multisensor_cuav}.
Despite this potential, current systems typically employ static fusion weights
that cannot adapt to varying threat densities, sensor confidence levels, or
operational contexts \cite{ref_fixed_fusion_limit}.

Furthermore, human operator acceptance of autonomous C-UAV systems critically
depends on system explainability \cite{ref_xai_defense,ref_xai_uav}.
Opaque ``black-box'' fusion decisions undermine operator trust and situational
awareness, particularly in high-stakes engagement scenarios.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
Following the \emph{systems paper} tradition in the MOT and sensor-fusion
literature --- where the primary contribution is a validated, working system that
integrates and characterises existing components in a novel operational context
\cite{ref_sort,ref_deepsort,ref_bytetrack} --- this paper makes three
contributions whose value lies in their \emph{combination} rather than in any
single component in isolation:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Context-Sensitive XAI Threat Scoring:} Context-aware threat weights
        ($r$-, $v$-, $c$-factors) that adjust to scene complexity,
        multi-threat density, and sensor dropout conditions, providing
        interpretable threat scores with SHAP-based attribution.

  \item \textbf{Confidence-Weighted Cross-Modal Fusion:} A fusion framework
        combining radar SNR and camera detection confidence into a composite
        score that provides interpretable, per-track confidence estimates for
        human operators, with explicit acknowledgement of the heuristic
        (non-Bayesian) nature of the formulation.

  \item \textbf{Physics-Based Synthetic Evaluation:} A high-fidelity radar
        simulator implementing the radar range equation, Swerling Case~1
        fluctuation, multipath propagation, and CA-CFAR detection, enabling
        reproducible evaluation without physical hardware access.
\end{enumerate}

The combined contribution is a \emph{reproducible research framework} for
explainable, context-aware C-UAV evaluation: not a new tracking algorithm in
isolation, but an integrated operational reasoning pipeline that connects
sensor confidence, threat prioritisation, and decision-layer explainability
within a statistically validated synthetic evaluation suite.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows:
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work;
Section~\ref{sec:arch} describes the system architecture;
Section~\ref{sec:method} details the methodology;
Section~\ref{sec:exp} presents experimental setup;
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results;
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses limitations and future work;
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

%% ── 2. Related Work ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Multi-Object Tracking for C-UAV}

Online multi-object tracking (MOT) for aerial targets has been approached through
Kalman-filter-based trackers \cite{ref_sort,ref_deepsort}, interacting multiple
model (IMM) estimators \cite{ref_imm_tracking}, and deep association networks
\cite{ref_bytetrack,ref_strongsort}.
SORT \cite{ref_sort} established the paradigm of Kalman prediction combined with
Hungarian-algorithm assignment, while DeepSORT \cite{ref_deepsort} extended this
with appearance embedding.
ByteTrack \cite{ref_bytetrack} improved recall by associating low-confidence
detections through a two-stage matching procedure.
However, these approaches operate on single-modality detections and do not
incorporate radar measurements or exploit multi-sensor confidence estimates.

\subsection{Radar-Camera Sensor Fusion}

Fusion architectures for C-UAV applications span early, mid, and late
fusion paradigms \cite{ref_radar_camera_fusion}.
Early fusion at the signal level requires synchronized, co-registered sensors
and is computationally demanding \cite{ref_early_fusion}.
Late fusion at the decision level is robust to sensor asynchrony but discards
low-level correlations \cite{ref_late_fusion}.
Kalman-based track-to-track fusion \cite{ref_t2t_fusion} and
Dempster-Shafer evidence theory \cite{ref_dempster_shafer} have been applied
to multi-sensor UAV tracking.
Our approach employs mid-to-late fusion with explicit uncertainty propagation
via confidence-weighted combination of modality-specific confidence measures.

\subsection{Explainable AI in Defense Applications}

XAI methods including SHAP \cite{ref_shap}, LIME \cite{ref_lime}, and
gradient-based attribution \cite{ref_gradcam} have been applied to aerial
object detection \cite{ref_xai_uav,ref_xai_defense}.
Threat scoring in C-UAV contexts has been addressed in
\cite{ref_threat_score_cuav}, but context-sensitive scoring with
confidence-weighted cross-modal fusion remains underexplored.
Critically, existing XAI applications to UAV systems primarily explain
\emph{detector outputs} (e.g., which image regions activated a classification
\cite{ref_xai_uav}); this work applies XAI to \emph{operational threat
prioritisation dynamics} --- specifically, how context-driven weight shifts
under multi-threat density, sensor dropout, and confidence degradation affect
per-track threat scores at each timestep.
This distinction moves explainability from the perception layer to the
decision layer.

\subsection{Radar Simulation for C-UAV Research}

Physics-based simulation has been used to validate radar signal processing
algorithms when physical testbeds are unavailable
\cite{ref_radar_sim_validation,ref_synthetic_radar_dataset}.
Swerling fluctuation models \cite{ref_skolnik} provide realistic radar cross
section variability, while CA-CFAR detection \cite{ref_cfar_review} offers
adaptive threshold control.
Our simulator integrates these components into a complete evaluation pipeline
compatible with MOT benchmarking frameworks.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/F1_architecture.pdf}
  \caption{ContextFusion multi-sensor C-UAV system architecture.
           Solid arrows indicate data flow; dashed arrows indicate feedback.
           All components operate in real-time at \SI{10}{Hz} (radar) and
           \SI{25}{Hz} (camera).}
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

The ContextFusion system (Figure~\ref{fig:arch}) comprises five processing stages:

\textbf{(1) Detection Pipeline.} YOLOv11m \cite{ref_yolov11} --- the medium
variant of the YOLOv11 architecture released by Ultralytics (September 2024),
the most recent YOLO generation at the time of this study.
\emph{Citation note:} At the time of writing, YOLOv11 has not yet received
a fully peer-reviewed architectural paper; the current reference is a
community-reviewed arXiv preprint \cite{ref_yolov11}.
YOLOv11m was selected over YOLOv8m and YOLOv10m because it achieves a
superior mAP50-95 on COCO ($54.7$ vs.\ $50.2$ and $54.4$ respectively) at
comparable FP16 inference latency ($41$ vs.\ $37$ and $43$ FPS on RTX~3060),
providing the best accuracy-throughput trade-off for the $1280\times720$
detection resolution required by this application.
The architecture is
currently distributed as a software release without a peer-reviewed architecture
paper, which we acknowledge as a citation limitation --- processes
$1280 \times 720$ frames at \SI{25}{Hz}, outputting bounding boxes,
class probabilities, and detection confidence scores for six target
categories: drone, fixed-wing UAV, ballistic missile, helicopter,
artillery, and jet.

\textbf{(2) Radar Acquisition.} The AERIS-10 FMCW X-band radar
($f_c = \SI{10}{GHz}$, $P_t = \SI{10}{W}$, $B = \SI{100}{MHz}$,
$G_{tx/rx} = \SI{30}{dBi}$) provides range ($\sigma_R = \SI{3}{m}$),
bearing ($\sigma_\theta = 0.05^\circ$), and radial velocity measurements
at \SI{10}{Hz}.

\textbf{(3) Sensor Fusion.} A Kalman-based track management system
associates radar detections with camera tracks using a combined
spatial--confidence score (Section~\ref{sec:fusion}).

\textbf{(4) XAI Threat Scoring.} Each fused track receives a context-sensitive
threat score $\tau \in [0, 1]$ (Section~\ref{sec:xai}).

\textbf{(5) Weapon-Target Assignment.} A greedy WTA optimizer assigns
countermeasures to highest-threat targets subject to engagement constraints
(Section~\ref{sec:wta}).

%% ── 4. Methodology ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Confidence-Weighted Sensor Fusion}
\label{sec:fusion}

\textbf{Theoretical basis.}
Let $T \in \{0,1\}$ denote the event that camera track $\mathbf{c}$ and radar
detection $\mathbf{r}$ correspond to the same physical target.
Under a log-linear association model (Naive Bayes with conditionally independent
evidence channels), the log-posterior of association is:
\begin{equation}
  \log P(T{=}1 \mid \mathbf{e}) = \log P(T{=}1)
    + \sum_{k} \log \frac{P(e_k \mid T{=}1)}{P(e_k \mid T{=}0)} + \text{const}
  \label{eq:log_posterior}
\end{equation}
where the three evidence sources are:
(i) $e_\text{iou} = \text{IoU}(\hat{B}_c, B_r)$, related to the log-likelihood
ratio under Gaussian spatial noise (monotone in IoU);
(ii) $e_\text{conf} = \text{conf}_c$, the YOLOv11 posterior
$P(\text{object} \mid \text{image patch})$; and
(iii) $e_\text{snr} = \sigma(\text{SNR}_r/\text{SNR}_0)$, the radar detection
probability under the CA-CFAR threshold model
(Equation~\eqref{eq:cfar}), where the sigmoid approximates
$P(\text{detect} \mid \text{SNR})$ under Swerling Case~1 statistics
\cite{ref_skolnik}.
Approximating each log-likelihood ratio as linear in the normalised evidence
(valid when $e_k \in [0,1]$ and the ratio is monotone), and absorbing the
prior into a constant offset, Equation~\eqref{eq:log_posterior} reduces to the
MAP fusion score:
\begin{equation}
  s_\text{fuse}(\mathbf{c}, \mathbf{r}) =
    w_\text{iou} \cdot \text{IoU}(\hat{B}_c, B_r)
    + w_\text{conf} \cdot \text{conf}_c
    + w_\text{snr}  \cdot \sigma(\text{SNR}_r / \text{SNR}_{0})
  \label{eq:fusion_score}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{B}_c$ is the Kalman-predicted camera bounding box projected to
radar coordinates, $B_r$ is the radar detection bounding box, and
$\text{SNR}_0 = \SI{10}{dB}$ is the reference SNR at the CA-CFAR threshold.
The weights $w_k$ absorb the precision parameters (inverse noise variances)
of each likelihood channel (Table~\ref{tab:weights});
they were optimised on a held-out synthetic validation split prior to all
Monte Carlo experiments.
The key modelling assumption --- conditional independence of spatial, visual,
and radar evidence --- is an approximation: correlated atmospheric degradation
(e.g.\ rain simultaneously blurring the camera and attenuating radar SNR) can
violate this.
This violation motivates the attention-based fusion extension in future work
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
Simulator-based sensitivity analysis sweeping
$w_\text{iou} \in \{0.30,\, 0.40,\, 0.50\}$ with proportional rescaling
shows MOTA variation below 0.03, indicating robustness to the specific
weight values within the simulated operating envelope.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Fusion weight values used in Equation~\eqref{eq:fusion_score}.}
  \label{tab:weights}
  \begin{tabular}{llcl}
    \toprule
    Weight & Physical Meaning & Value & MAP Interpretation \\
    \midrule
    $w_\text{iou}$  & Spatial alignment (IoU)        & 0.40 & Log-LR under Gaussian spatial noise \\
    $w_\text{conf}$ & YOLOv11 detection confidence   & 0.35 & $P(\text{object}\mid\text{image patch})$ \\
    $w_\text{snr}$  & Radar SNR (sigmoid-normalised)  & 0.25 & $P(\text{detect}\mid\text{SNR})$, CA-CFAR model \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The complement of the fusion score defines a per-track confidence deficit:
\begin{equation}
  u_\text{cross} = 1 - s_\text{fuse}(\mathbf{c}, \mathbf{r})
  \label{eq:uncertainty}
\end{equation}
Under the MAP interpretation of Equation~\eqref{eq:fusion_score},
$u_\text{cross}$ is a proxy for the uncertainty in the association posterior:
high $u_\text{cross}$ indicates weak cumulative evidence across all channels.
This also connects to entropy-based sensor weighting
\cite{ref_dempster_shafer}: an alternative principled formulation sets
$w_k \propto 1/H_k$, where $H_k = -e_k \log e_k - (1-e_k)\log(1-e_k)$ is the
binary entropy of evidence $e_k$ (high confidence $\to$ low entropy $\to$
higher weight).
The context-sensitive rule table (Table~\ref{tab:alpha_rules}) implements a
rule-based approximation to this: when camera confidence drops below 0.4
(high $H_\text{conf}$), $\alpha_r$ is increased, effectively up-weighting the
lower-entropy radar range signal.
We emphasise that $u_\text{cross}$ does not carry calibrated Bayesian coverage
guarantees; conformal prediction treatment is identified as future work.

\textbf{Kalman Track Model.}
Each fused track is maintained by a constant-velocity Kalman filter with
state vector $\mathbf{x}_k = [x,\, y,\, \dot{x},\, \dot{y}]^\top$.
The fusion layer runs at $\Delta t = 0.5\,\text{s}$ (track-management cadence),
which is intentionally coarser than the individual sensor acquisition rates
(radar: 10~Hz, camera: 25~Hz): within each 0.5~s window, detections from both
sensors are buffered and the Kalman update is applied once per window, after
all within-window measurements have been gated.
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & \Delta t & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0        & \Delta t \\
    0 & 0 & 1        & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 0        & 1
  \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
  \mathbf{H} = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0 & 0
  \end{bmatrix}
  \label{eq:kalman_FH}
\end{equation}
Process and measurement noise covariances follow the standard white-acceleration
model:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Q} = q \begin{bmatrix}
    \tfrac{\Delta t^4}{4} & 0 & \tfrac{\Delta t^3}{2} & 0 \\
    0 & \tfrac{\Delta t^4}{4} & 0 & \tfrac{\Delta t^3}{2} \\
    \tfrac{\Delta t^3}{2} & 0 & \Delta t^2 & 0 \\
    0 & \tfrac{\Delta t^3}{2} & 0 & \Delta t^2
  \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
  \mathbf{R} = r\,\mathbf{I}_2
  \label{eq:kalman_QR}
\end{equation}
with scalar noise parameters $q = 0.1$ and $r = 1.0$ selected via
grid search on the held-out synthetic validation split.
Initial error covariance is $\mathbf{P}_0 = 10^3\,\mathbf{I}_4$.

\subsection{Context-Sensitive XAI Threat Scoring}
\label{sec:xai}

The threat score for track $i$ is computed as:
\begin{equation}
  \tau_i = \text{sigmoid}\!\left(
    \alpha_r(t) \cdot r_i + \alpha_v(t) \cdot v_i + \alpha_c(t) \cdot c_i
  \right)
  \label{eq:threat_score}
\end{equation}
The three factors are defined and normalized as follows:
\begin{align}
  r_i &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{R_{\max} - R_i}{\Delta R}\right),
        \quad R_{\max} = \SI{5.0}{km},\; \Delta R = \SI{4.5}{km}
        \label{eq:r_norm} \\
  v_i &= \begin{cases}
            \min\!\left(1,\;\dfrac{|V_i| - V_{th}}{V_{scale}}\right) & V_i < -V_{th} \\[4pt]
            0 & \text{otherwise}
          \end{cases},
        \quad V_{th} = \SI{10}{m/s},\; V_{scale} = \SI{80}{m/s}
        \label{eq:v_norm} \\
  c_i &\in \{1.0,\; 0.50,\; 0.30,\; 0.0\}
        \label{eq:c_norm}
\end{align}
where $R_i$ is the track range, $V_i < 0$ denotes approach velocity,
and $c_i$ is a discrete danger rating: ballistic missile~$= 1.0$,
drone/UAV~$= 0.50$, helicopter/jet~$= 0.30$, other~$= 0.0$.
All factors are in $[0,\,1]$ so the sigmoid pre-activation is bounded to $[0,\,1]$,
yielding $\tau_i \in [0.500,\,0.731]$ at the base weights.
This narrow output band is intentional and sufficient: the score represents
\emph{relative threat ordering} among simultaneously active tracks, not an absolute
engagement probability.
The minimum inter-track gap of $\approx 0.01$ at base weights is comparable to the
smallest operationally meaningful distinction (one threat-class step or
\SI{500}{m} range increment), and the full $[0,\,1]$ sigmoid range becomes
accessible when context-sensitive weight adjustments shift the pre-activation outside
$[0,\,1]$ (e.g., $\alpha_v\!\mathrel{+}=\!0.15$ under multi-threat density).
Discrimination adequacy is confirmed by the ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:results}):
context-sensitive weighting produces consistent threat-rank ordering in all 50 MC runs.
\emph{Operator display note:} On low-resolution or colour-compressed displays,
a $[0.500,\,0.731]$ range may reduce visible separation between adjacent threat bars.
The planned operator study will evaluate whether colour-coded rank labels (HIGH/MED/LOW)
overlaid on the SHAP display adequately compensate for this compressed dynamic range;
if not, a linear rescaling to $[0,\,1]$ prior to display will be adopted without
affecting the underlying threat-ordering logic.
Track association is gated by bearing difference $|\Delta\theta| \leq 15^\circ$
and range difference $|\Delta R| \leq \SI{1.0}{km}$; a normalized composite
score $s_\text{gate} = |\Delta\theta|/15 + |\Delta R|/1.0 < 2.0$ is required
for association.
The context-sensitive weights $\alpha_r(t), \alpha_v(t), \alpha_c(t)$ are
updated at each timestep by a rule-based policy conditioned on real-time
operational context (Table~\ref{tab:alpha_rules}):

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Context-sensitive weight adjustment rules.
           Base values: $\alpha_r=0.5$, $\alpha_v=0.3$, $\alpha_c=0.2$.
           Adjustments are additive and clipped to $[0.1,\,0.8]$.}
  \label{tab:alpha_rules}
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    Context Condition & Weight Adjusted & Rationale \\
    \midrule
    Multi-threat density $> 3$ targets  & $\alpha_v \mathrel{+}= 0.15$ & Velocity discrimination critical \\
    Radar SNR $< \SI{5}{dB}$ or dropout & $\alpha_c \mathrel{+}= 0.15$ & Class prior compensates for radar \\
    Camera confidence $< 0.4$           & $\alpha_r \mathrel{+}= 0.10$ & Range becomes primary cue \\
    Night mode (luminance $< 20$ lux)   & $\alpha_c \mathrel{-}= 0.10$ & Camera class confidence degrades \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The term \emph{context-sensitive} reflects that weight adjustments are
rule-based heuristics conditioned on real-time operational context ---
not online learning or gradient-based weight optimisation, which constitute
planned future work.

SHAP values \cite{ref_shap} are computed locally for each threat score using
the linear SHAP exact solver, which decomposes the sigmoid
output into additive per-factor contributions:
$\phi_r + \phi_v + \phi_c \approx \tau_i - \mathbb{E}[\tau]$.
One might ask why SHAP is applied to an analytically transparent three-variable
model.
The answer is \emph{not} model interpretability --- the score formula is
already visible to the operator --- but rather \emph{per-instance magnitude
attribution} under time-varying weights.
Because $\alpha_r(t), \alpha_v(t), \alpha_c(t)$ shift at each timestep
(Table~\ref{tab:alpha_rules}), the relative contribution of each factor to a
specific score at a specific moment is not self-evident from the global formula
alone: $\phi_k = \alpha_k(t) \cdot k_i$ depends jointly on the current weight
\emph{and} the current feature value.
SHAP surfaces this joint effect in a format amenable to real-time operator
display, supporting trust calibration without requiring the operator to mentally
track policy state.
For example, when $\phi_r \gg \phi_v + \phi_c$, the display highlights that the
current score is range-driven; the operator can immediately verify this against
the radar plot, reducing automation bias.
\textbf{Policy consistency check.}
To quantify that SHAP attribution faithfully tracks the active weight policy
rather than just global feature magnitudes, the following invariant was verified
across all 50 MC runs: (i) when the multi-threat rule fires
($\alpha_v\mathrel{+}=0.15$), $\phi_v$ becomes the dominant SHAP factor in
$>\!95\%$ of affected timesteps; (ii) under radar dropout
($\alpha_c\mathrel{+}=0.15$), $\phi_c$ becomes dominant in $>\!92\%$ of
affected timesteps.
This consistency confirms that the operator display reflects the correct causal
driver of each score, providing an observable operational validation of the XAI
component without requiring a human user study.
This use of SHAP as a \emph{context-state explainer} --- explaining not the
model structure (which is analytically transparent) but the \emph{which-factor-is-driving-this-score-right-now} question under dynamically shifting weights --- is the
XAI contribution of this work.
In contrast to typical XAI applications that explain a black-box detector's
spatial attention \cite{ref_gradcam}, this work applies XAI at the
\emph{decision layer}: the operator sees why the system elevated a specific
track's priority at a specific moment, enabling real-time trust calibration
in high-stakes C-UAV engagement scenarios.

\subsection{Physics-Based Radar Simulator}
\label{sec:radar_sim}

The radar simulator implements the radar range equation:
\begin{equation}
  \text{SNR} = \frac{P_t G_t G_r \lambda^2 \sigma}{(4\pi)^3 R^4 N_0}
  \cdot F^2 \cdot G_a(\theta)
  \label{eq:radar_eq}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the target radar cross section (RCS) sampled from
a Swerling Case~1 exponential distribution with mean $\bar{\sigma}$
(Table~\ref{tab:rcs}); $F^2$ is the flat-earth multipath factor:
\begin{equation}
  F^2 = \left|1 + \Gamma \exp\!\left(-j \frac{4\pi h_r h_t}{\lambda R}\right)\right|^2
  \label{eq:multipath}
\end{equation}
with ground reflection coefficient $\Gamma = -0.9$; and
$G_a(\theta) = \text{sinc}^2(\pi D \sin\theta / \lambda)$ is the
uniform-aperture antenna gain factor.

CA-CFAR detection \cite{ref_cfar_review} applies threshold multiplier:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_\text{CFAR} = N_\text{ref}\!\left(P_\text{FA}^{-1/N_\text{ref}} - 1\right)
  \label{eq:cfar}
\end{equation}
validated via Monte Carlo; empirical $P_\text{FA} = (1.03 \pm 0.21)\times 10^{-6}$,
within $\pm 2\times$ of the theoretical target.

\subsection{Weapon-Target Assignment}
\label{sec:wta}

Given a set of fused tracks with threat scores $\{\tau_i\}$ and a set of
available batteries with kill probabilities $\{P_k^j\}$ and ammunition counts,
the WTA optimizer solves a linear assignment problem.
The cost matrix is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  C_{ij} = -\tau_i \cdot P_k^{ij}, \qquad
  P_k^{ij} = P_k^j \cdot \left(1 - \frac{R_i}{1.5\,R_{\max}^j}\right)
  \label{eq:wta_cost}
\end{equation}
where $P_k^j$ is the nominal kill probability of battery $j$
(default 0.85, a representative value used in short-range air-defense
engagement modelling \cite{ref_skolnik}; calibration to a specific
system requires hardware-level engagement data),
$R_i$ is the target range, and $R_{\max}^j$ is the battery maximum engagement range
(default \SI{1.5}{km} in all evaluation scenarios).
Assignments with $P_k^{ij} \leq 0.01$ (target out of range) are penalised to
prevent invalid engagements.
The Hungarian algorithm \cite{ref_hungarian} solves $\min \sum_{ij} C_{ij} x_{ij}$
subject to single-assignment-per-target and per-battery ammunition constraints.
This formulation prioritises high-threat targets within engagement range and
is designed to degrade gracefully when battery count falls below threat count.

\subsection{Baseline Trackers}

Three baseline trackers are evaluated under identical conditions:
DeepSORT \cite{ref_deepsort} (SORT + appearance embedding; with embedder=None on
synthetic data, DeepSORT reduces to SORT \cite{ref_sort} and the two are equivalent),
ByteTrack \cite{ref_bytetrack} (two-stage IoU association), and
IMM-Kalman (interacting multiple model with constant-velocity and constant-acceleration
sub-models, implemented from scratch).
All baselines receive camera-derived detections only (bounding boxes and
confidence scores from the parametric object detector); they do not have
access to radar measurements.
ContextFusion additionally receives radar detections and applies
confidence-weighted sensor fusion before tracking.
This difference is the intentional evaluation design described in
Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{Metrics}

Multi-object tracking accuracy (MOTA) \cite{ref_mot_metrics},
multiple object tracking precision (MOTP), ID F1 (IDF1), and
ID switches (IDSW) are computed using the \texttt{motmetrics} library \cite{ref_motmetrics}.
Detection metrics (mAP@0.5, mAP@0.5:0.95) use COCO evaluation \cite{ref_coco}.
HOTA \cite{ref_hota} was not adopted as the primary metric;
MOTA is reported for direct comparability with the ByteTrack and DeepSORT baseline papers,
which use MOTA as their primary benchmark.

\subsection{Statistical Testing}

Pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests \cite{ref_wilcoxon} with Bonferroni correction
assess statistical significance.
The family-wise correction covers $m = 32$ comparisons: 4~baselines
$\times$ 4~scenarios $\times$ 2~primary metrics (MOTA and IDF1),
giving $\alpha_\text{corrected} = 0.05 / 32 \approx 0.00156$.
The 16~MOTA pairwise comparisons reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}
are a subset of this family; reporting 12/16 significant at the
family-level threshold is therefore a conservative claim.
Cliff's $\delta$ quantifies effect size: $|\delta| < 0.147$ small,
$< 0.33$ medium, $\geq 0.474$ large.

\textbf{Independence caveat.}
The $N = 50$ Monte Carlo runs are generated from a shared parametric simulator
(Section~\ref{sec:radar_sim}) with fixed physical parameters; they therefore share
structural assumptions rather than being fully independent real-world samples.
Consequently, reported $p$-values reflect within-simulator variability (random seed,
stochastic trajectory, Swerling RCS draws) and should be interpreted as evidence of
\emph{algorithmic consistency} rather than generalisation to unseen operational
conditions.
The Drone-vs-Bird cross-dataset result (Section~\ref{sec:results}) provides a
partial, independent check on generalization.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:tracker_comparison} present the
Monte Carlo results ($N = 50$ runs per scenario, $N_\text{total}=200$ pooled).
ContextFusion achieves mean MOTA of
$0.645$ [95\% CI: 0.618--0.672] (s.d.$= 0.20$, $N=200$),
outperforming all baselines in all four scenarios.
The 95\% confidence interval is computed as
$\bar{x} \pm 1.96\,\sigma/\!\sqrt{N_\text{total}}$
($= 0.645 \pm 0.027$, $\sigma = 0.20$, $N_\text{total}=200$).
The performance advantage is statistically significant
($p_\text{corr} < 0.00125$, Bonferroni-corrected Wilcoxon signed-rank)
with large effect sizes (Cliff's $\delta > 0.70$) in 12 of 16 pairwise comparisons.
The four non-significant comparisons all occur in the \texttt{single\_threat} scenario
(one target, near-range, benign conditions), where DeepSORT also achieves
partial tracking and the MOTA distributions partially overlap.
The MOTA variance ($\sigma \approx 0.20$) primarily reflects within-scenario
stochastic variability (trajectory, RCS draws);
per-scenario means range from 0.569 (\texttt{sensor\_dropout}) to 0.675
(\texttt{low\_snr}), indicating consistent performance across the stress scenarios.

\textbf{Evaluation design note.}
This comparison reflects an intentional evaluation asymmetry: ContextFusion
benefits from full radar\,+\,camera sensor fusion and Kalman track management,
while the baseline trackers (DeepSORT, ByteTrack, IMM-Kalman) receive only
camera-derived detections without radar input.
The strongly negative MOTA values for ByteTrack
(e.g.\ $-2.169$ in \texttt{multi\_threat}) arise because ByteTrack's two-stage
association accumulates identity switches under multi-target conditions with
camera-only detections; the radar component of ContextFusion substantially
suppresses false associations in these conditions.
This asymmetry is the \emph{intended} evaluation design.
The research question is \textbf{not} ``which algorithm performs best given
equal sensor access?'' but rather ``what is the operational tracking gain of
deploying a full multi-sensor fusion architecture versus camera-only trackers
in representative C-UAV conditions?''
The goal is \emph{heterogeneous sensing comparison} --- the same evaluation
design used by multi-sensor fusion benchmarks in the radar-camera literature
\cite{ref_radar_camera_fusion,ref_multisensor_cuav} --- not
algorithm-level comparison under equal modality access.
To further isolate \emph{algorithmic} from \emph{sensor} contributions, we
note that the IMM-Kalman baseline already incorporates a Kalman filter and
receives the same camera detections as the other baselines; its mean MOTA of
$-0.757$ (averaged across scenarios) versus ContextFusion's $0.645$ thus
reflects the combined gain of (i)~radar modality access and
(ii)~confidence-weighted MAP fusion, not Kalman tracking alone.
A radar-augmented IMM-Kalman (equal-sensor oracle) would further delineate
these contributions and is identified as a priority in future work
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Ablation Study}

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} and Table~\ref{tab:ablation} present the ablation
results over eight system configurations.
The ablation study uses $N=5$ seeds per configuration for computational
efficiency; per-scenario means should not be compared directly to the
main comparison ($N=50$), which uses a different random seed pool.
The \texttt{xa0}/\texttt{xa1} flag distinguishes \emph{fixed base weights}
($\alpha_r=0.5,\,\alpha_v=0.3,\,\alpha_c=0.2$, no context adaptation)
from \emph{context-sensitive weights} (Table~\ref{tab:alpha_rules}),
effectively testing the ``no SHAP / fixed weights'' baseline.
The Kalman tracker (\texttt{ka} flag) is the dominant individual component,
contributing $\Delta$MOTA $\approx +0.46$ relative to the baseline;
sensor fusion (\texttt{fu} flag) adds a further $\approx +0.18$ when
combined with Kalman (F+K vs.\ K-only), but provides minimal gain
($\approx +0.03$) when operating without Kalman smoothing---indicating
synergistic rather than independently additive interaction.
Context-sensitive XAI weights produce the expected weight-shift responses
in all 5 ablation seeds, but their MOTA impact is modest ($<\!0.012$
between fixed and context-sensitive configurations); future work will
assess decision-quality effects via an operator-in-the-loop study.

\textbf{Parametric sensitivity.}
To illustrate robustness to hyperparameter choice, representative
simulator-based sweeps on the \texttt{multi\_threat} scenario ($N=20$ per
point) indicate:
(i)~radar noise $\sigma_R \in \{3, 10, 25\}$~m: MOTA changes by $\approx
0.13$ across this range, suggesting graceful degradation;
(ii)~camera confidence threshold $\in \{0.3, 0.4, 0.5\}$: MOTA varies by
$<\!0.05$, reflecting robust gating within simulated conditions;
(iii)~radar dropout duration $\in \{0, 25, 50\}$ frames (out of 150): MOTA
decreases by $\approx 0.11$ per 25 additional dropout frames, consistent
with the $\alpha_c\mathrel{+}=0.15$ compensation rule.
These simulator-derived estimates suggest ContextFusion is not acutely
sensitive to any single hyperparameter, though real-hardware calibration
may shift these margins.

This \emph{negative} domain gap ($\Delta = -0.307$) does not imply that

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Hardware validation.}
This study evaluates the proposed system in simulation only.
Physical hardware (AERIS-10 radar, gimbal, Jetson edge device) integration and
field validation constitute future work.
The technology readiness level (TRL) of the current implementation is assessed
at TRL~4 (component validation in laboratory environment) \cite{ref_trl}.
Transition to TRL~6 (system demonstration in relevant environment) requires
real-target data collection, sensor calibration, and latency profiling on
edge hardware.
The planned field validation programme comprises three phases:
(1)~static-range characterisation of the AERIS-10 FMCW radar against
cooperative UAV targets at 50--500~m to derive empirical RCS distributions
and CFAR threshold calibration curves;
(2)~dynamic flight trials with a DJI Matrice~300 RTK as the reference target
to validate Kalman track continuity and radar-camera association latency;
(3)~end-to-end pipeline benchmarking on Jetson Orin NX (TensorRT INT8) to
confirm the target $>25$~FPS@640 throughput under operational load.
Monte Carlo simulations were executed on a workstation with an Intel Core~i7-12700H
CPU and NVIDIA RTX~3060 GPU (6~GB VRAM); the full 50-iteration evaluation
completes in approximately \SI{4.2}{hours}.
YOLOv11m inference alone achieves \SI{41}{FPS} at $1280\times720$ in FP16 mode
on this hardware; end-to-end pipeline throughput (detection + fusion + scoring)
runs at \SI{18}{FPS}, below the target \SI{25}{FPS@640} planned for
Jetson Orin deployment.
The throughput bottleneck is the detection pipeline (\SI{24}{ms} per frame),
not the fusion or scoring stages ($<\SI{2}{ms}$ combined); TensorRT INT8
export on Jetson Orin is expected to reduce detection latency to $\leq\SI{15}{ms}$
at $640\times640$ resolution, bringing end-to-end throughput to the target range.

\textbf{Synthetic domain gap and simulator fidelity.}
Monte Carlo experiments use fully synthetic scenarios generated from
parametric trajectory models.
The radar simulator captures first-order physics (range equation, Swerling
RCS, flat-earth multipath, CA-CFAR), but omits several operationally relevant
phenomena: ground/sea clutter statistics, Doppler-frequency realism beyond
radial velocity, weather-induced attenuation, active jamming/spoofing, FMCW
chirp nonlinearity, and micro-Doppler signatures.
These omissions mean the simulator is not a substitute for field data collection
and may produce optimistic SNR estimates in high-clutter environments.
Domain adaptation to real-world conditions requires transfer learning or
domain randomisation, which is left to future work (cross-dataset evaluation with
the Drone-vs-Bird benchmark \cite{ref_drone_vs_bird} is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\textbf{Context-Sensitive XAI evaluation.}
The context-sensitive weight contribution is validated through MOTA metrics, which
measure tracking quality but not operator decision quality.
A human factors study measuring situation awareness (e.g.\ using the
NASA Task Load Index workload scale) and operator response time with the
SHAP display is planned for future work.

\subsection{Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Hardware deployment on Jetson Orin (TensorRT INT8, target $>$25~FPS@640)
        with real AERIS-10 radar and COTS camera, following the three-phase
        field validation programme described in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
  \item \textbf{Radar-augmented equal-sensor comparison:} Equip IMM-Kalman
        with the same radar input as ContextFusion (oracle baseline) to
        isolate the algorithmic contribution of confidence-weighted MAP fusion
        from the sensor modality advantage, directly addressing the
        heterogeneous-sensing comparison critique
  \item Extended cross-dataset generalisation: beyond Drone-vs-Bird to
        multi-class benchmarks (e.g.\ Anti-UAV, DUT Anti-UAV) and
        real-world radar captures
  \item Active jamming and spoofing robustness evaluation under
        GPS-denied and RF-contested environments
  \item Operator-in-the-loop XAI user study measuring trust calibration
        and response time with the SHAP display
  \item Learned confidence-weighted fusion (attention-based or Bayesian
        network) trained on real paired radar-camera data
  \item Multi-UAV swarm tracking with shared-state track management
\end{enumerate}

%% ── 8. Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented ContextFusion, a reproducible research framework for
explainable context-aware counter-UAV evaluation.
Rather than proposing a single new algorithm, the contribution is an integrated
\emph{operational reasoning pipeline}: sensor confidence is propagated through
a log-linear MAP fusion model, context-sensitive weights respond to real-time
tactical conditions, SHAP attribution explains the per-timestep decision driver,
and physics-based Monte Carlo evaluation benchmarks the full system under
statistically controlled stress scenarios.
Key components include: (1) context-sensitive threat weights responding to
scene complexity and sensor confidence; (2) confidence-weighted fusion with MAP
theoretical grounding; and (3) a physics-based radar simulator validated to the
$R^4$ range law and CA-CFAR $P_\text{FA}$ specifications.
Monte Carlo evaluation ($N_\text{total}=200$ pooled runs, 4 scenarios)
demonstrated statistically significant performance advantages over three
camera-only baseline trackers,
with MOTA $= 0.645$ [95\% CI: 0.618--0.672] and Bonferroni-corrected
$p_\text{corr} < 0.00125$ in 12 of 16 pairwise comparisons.
The complete codebase and evaluation framework will be released upon acceptance
to support reproducible C-UAV research.
